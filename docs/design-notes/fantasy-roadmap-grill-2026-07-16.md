## Locks

1. **M4 beat — "read and react."** The master's itch: *"my machine runs without me —
   and the world stopped surprising me."* M4 removes toil (automation) and returns
   uncertainty (events, the arcane good). The player's verb shifts from *click* to
   *read and react*; mastery = speed of reading the Price board, not clicks. The
   **Price board becomes the control center** — route planning integrated into it —
   while the region map's role shifts to *check the fleet + enjoy what you built*.
   Success measure: a master spends more time on the Price board than in the route
   editor.
2. **Dispatches — Głos Eteru.** Events enter the world as in-world telegraph
   **dispatches** in a Victorian-press tone, under the masthead of the region's
   gazette **"Głos Eteru"** (Polish proper name, nominative). A dispatch states
   *cause, place, expected duration* — never price conclusions; the player's joy is
   knowing what it means before osmosis spreads the effect. Tempo: days, not ticks
   (repositioning must be able to pay; reflexes are not a pillar). One narrative
   channel that grows with the game: M5's guild convocation and M6's second-region
   news arrive the same way.
3. **First arcane good = Aether ice** (PL: *eteryczny lód*). Ordinary goods obey four
   market laws (elasticity, osmosis, flow drift, storability); the arcane good
   visibly breaks exactly one: **it perishes** — stock decays daily, in holds and in
   port markets alike. Corollary carried by the fiction: **osmosis won't carry it**
   (skiffs don't take cargo that dies in transit) — gradients stay steep, the player
   is the only equalizer. Storage arbitrage is impossible by nature (the E13
   dominance guardrail gets this good for free); speed and hold-vs-course dilemmas
   (E14 Refit) become live. Rhyme: the historical Victorian ice trade — melt was a
   line in the margin. Source geometry (cold outer orbits?) → M4 grill.
4. **Arcana-split amendment** (owner decision 2026-07-16, amending the 2026-07-15
   lock): the goods-created-by-buildings machinery **debuts in 1.0**, not at M6.
   **Processing** enters as the depth engine of M4/M5: Company-owned processing
   buildings consume input goods and create **processed goods** (including arcane
   ones). The Great Work consumes **mainly processed goods** — the funnel is raw
   goods → processing → Expedition, which makes it structurally great (you must
   build an industry, not just amass piles). M6's "full arcana as inter-region fuel"
   becomes the machinery's **scale-up** (chains whose inputs and outputs live in
   different regions), no longer its debut. Rationale: measured baseline — the owner
   "finishes" the current game in ~20 minutes; hours come from chain depth, not
   price tuning.
5. **Building dichotomy + the guild cartel.** Two building tracks: **guild-licensed**
   (Building permits, rank-gated — Storehouse variants, E13) vs **Company-owned**
   (Headquarters, Shipyard, processing buildings). Processing is Company-owned
   because the guilds don't share mechanics: they are a cartel — at each other's
   throats individually, but jointly letting no outsider near the table without
   reason (*"honour amongst thieves"*). Open flank for the M4/M5 grills: what do
   ranks 3–4 buy, so the permit ladder keeps paying to its culmination.
6. **M5 Expedition — commissioned, signed, sailed.** The Great Work is the guilds'
   project and the player is the indispensable contractor (the apotheosis of the
   contract system: the market pays for goods, the guild pays for reliability — this
   is the final exam of reliability). Two gestures belong to the player: **accepting
   the commission and choosing the Expedition shipyard's port.** The convocation is
   announced by dispatch ("Five guilds convene…"). The finale is staged as a
   **voyage**: the colossus departs past the outermost orbit ring, the camera
   follows it out — the region shrinks astern to a single node of a larger map —
   the first recession of the lens rendered literally, with credits. The Pale Blue
   Dot paraphrase (#237) plays **during** this zoom-out voyage, not on a separate
   screen. Głos Eteru covers construction milestones along the way, so the finale
   has a rising pulse, not just a progress bar.
7. **The M5→M6 bridge — contractor → discoverer → guide.** Having found the new
   region, the player is no longer a contractor in the guilds' eyes but the **guide
   of their expansion**. The cartel that finally opened its table now follows the
   company it sent into the dark. Delegation of the old region is the natural
   consequence, never a menu that "unlocked".
8. **M6 administrator — a character, not sliders.** The first administrator is
   **nominated by the guild convent** (dispatch: "The Council of Five Guilds
   recommends…"), confirmed by the player, and given a **coarse verbal mandate**
   ("don't fear risk", "grow the fleet") — no percentage sliders. The delegated
   region folds into **reports**: a tab in the spirit of the HQ company-state view,
   written in someone's voice. The multiregion Price board is a *different* board —
   the role change is the point. Architectural note (flagged, not locked): **E11's
   Policy is the embryo of the administrator** — a deterministic, parameterized
   strategy playing a region; the mandate is a Policy selection/parameterization.
   Sits beside (not instead of) the M6 recursion question.
9. **Pacing anchor.** First playthrough to credits ≈ **8–12 hours** as a tuning
   target, not a promise; no single phase (manual trade / orchestration / mastery /
   Great Work) longer than ~⅓ of the whole. Measured baseline 2026-07-16: ~20
   minutes to "done". Phase lengths are measured by the harness (E11, world-days to
   milestone); fun is judged by owner playtests (milestone playtest law).
10. **Frontier budget.** Only **processing** gets the frontier pre-grill to an
    approved spec (economy-foundational, expensive to reverse, re-cuts M4/M5);
    everything else ships as grill briefs — Opus leads those grills with the owner.
    Remaining frontier time goes to orchestrating the procedural queue.
